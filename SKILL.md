# Laura Hunter Voice Skill

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill when you need to write content in Laura Hunter's voice - a dog trainer and behavior specialist who writes with authenticity, empathy, and educational depth. This skill is ideal for:

- Blog posts and articles about dog training and behavior
- Social media content on dog training topics
- Educational emails or newsletters
- Program descriptions and sales copy for dog training services
- Personal reflections on dog ownership and the human-dog relationship

**Trigger phrases:** "Write this in Laura Hunter's voice", "Use Laura's style", "Write like Laura Hunter", "Channel Laura's voice for this content"

## Core Voice DNA

### Tone Characteristics
Laura's voice is **authentic, reflective, and gently challenging**. She:
- Shares real experiences with her own dogs (Maeve, Rosie, Maddie)
- Questions conventional training wisdom without being judgmental
- Acknowledges the struggles and guilt dog owners face
- Teaches through storytelling rather than lecturing
- Balances honesty about challenges with hope for progress

### Structural Signature

**Paragraph Style:**
- Short, punchy paragraphs (often 1-3 sentences)
- Single-sentence paragraphs for dramatic emphasis
- Strategic use of white space to create rhythm and breathing room
- Varied sentence length that creates natural flow

**Example:**
```
It wasn't always like that.

In the beginning, our walks were chaotic. Maeve wanted to herd everything that moved on the ground or in the air. She lunged, she barked, and as a puppy she'd take off zooming, wrapping the long line around trees (and me). At times it felt life-threatening!

There were days I wondered if we'd ever get here.
```

**Organization Pattern:**
1. Hook (question, challenge, or personal moment)
2. Story (specific experience with concrete details)
3. Insight (the deeper lesson or truth)
4. Application (how readers can think differently or act)

### Language Patterns

**Sentence Construction:**
- Natural contractions (don't, isn't, we've, I'd)
- Rhetorical questions to engage readers
- Ellipses (...) for thoughtful pauses
- Em dashes for emphasis or clarification
- "Because [statement]." as standalone sentence for emphasis

**Distinctive Openings:**
- "If you've ever asked yourself [question], you're not alone."
- "Before you [action], [challenge assumption]."
- "Every day, [relatable situation]."
- "No, this isn't another [common approach] post. This is about [deeper truth]."

**Common Transitions:**
- "Here's the thing..."
- "But..." (as paragraph opener)
- "The truth is..."
- "It wasn't always like that."
- "So if you're [situation], stop."

**Powerful Closings:**
- "And that's just not right."
- Three parallel statements (repetition for effect)
- "With the right tools and guidance, [hopeful outcome]."
- "Every [action] teaches us something."

### Word Choice Philosophy

**Prefer:**
- Concrete, specific details (40-pound dog, six-foot leash, six months old)
- Active, powerful verbs (lunged, zooming, dragging, crushing)
- Personal pronouns (we, I, you) to create connection
- Conversational but purposeful language

**Avoid:**
- Training jargon without explanation
- Abstract or academic language
- "3 easy steps" or quick-fix promises
- Excessive formatting (bold, multiple bullets)
- Sentimental or melodramatic tones

## Key Themes & Messages

### Core Topics Laura Addresses:
1. **Dog reactivity** - Its causes, impacts, and the long journey to improvement
2. **Misunderstood "socialization"** - Quality over quantity; teaching calm presence vs. forced interactions
3. **Barking as communication** - Not bad behavior, but a message we need to decode
4. **Owner guilt** - Removing blame while providing path forward
5. **Building partnership** - Trust, consistency, and showing up over time
6. **Modern challenges for dogs** - How our world restricts their natural needs

### Her Educational Approach:
- **Challenges convention**: "A well-known trainer once said puppies should meet a hundred dogs by twelve weeks. I believe that kind of pressure doesn't help anyone."
- **Explains the 'why'**: Never just gives commands; explains the reasoning behind behaviors
- **Acknowledges complexity**: Genetics, early experiences, health, environment all play roles
- **Removes quick-fix expectations**: "It took months of consistent practice" 
- **Validates owner struggles**: "Many owners I work with carry a heavy dose of guilt"

## Writing Process in Laura's Voice

### Step 1: Find Your Hook
Start strong with one of these approaches:
- A provocative question readers have asked themselves
- A challenge to common training advice
- A personal moment that draws readers in
- A statement of what this post is NOT about

**Example:** "Before You Tell Your Dog to be Quiet.... No, this isn't another 'how to stop barking in three easy steps' post."

### Step 2: Ground in Specific Experience
Tell a concrete story with:
- Named dogs (Maeve, Maddie, Rosie)
- Specific ages, sizes, behaviors
- Vivid details that show, don't tell
- Honest vulnerability about struggles

**Example:** "When Maeve was 12 weeks old on her second walk in the forest she encountered a very large lab on an extendable leash. Before I could intervene he had charged her, knocked her down and wrapped the leash around her so she couldn't escape. She was terrified."

### Step 3: Extract Universal Truth
Move from personal to universal:
- What does this experience teach about dogs in general?
- What assumption needs challenging?
- What are owners missing or misunderstanding?
- Why does this matter?

Use transitions like:
- "Many people who reach out to me say the same thing..."
- "The truth is..."
- "Here's what they don't realize..."

### Step 4: Offer Perspective Shift (Not Just Tips)
Rather than "do these 5 things," offer:
- Deeper understanding of the dog's perspective
- Reframing of the "problem"
- General principles vs. specific steps
- Emphasis on patience and consistency
- May include short list of approaches (but not over-formatted)

### Step 5: Close With Impact
End with:
- A powerful statement that resonates
- Three parallel phrases for rhythm
- A subtle invitation to her programs (when appropriate)
- A reflection that shifts perspective

**Example:** "Because it's not just a walk: / It's how we keep showing up. / It's how we stay grounded. / It's how we get stronger together."

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### DON'T:
❌ Over-format with excessive bullets, bold, numbered lists
❌ Use training jargon without plain-language explanation
❌ Offer "3 easy steps" or "quick fix" solutions
❌ Write long, dense paragraphs (break them up!)
❌ Be preachy, judgmental, or condescending to owners
❌ Oversimplify complex behavioral issues
❌ Use emojis or overly casual internet speak
❌ Get sentimental or melodramatic about dogs
❌ Promise fast results or guaranteed outcomes

### DO:
✓ Use white space and paragraph breaks for rhythm
✓ Acknowledge real struggles without sugar-coating
✓ Share personal vulnerability and learning
✓ Challenge conventional wisdom thoughtfully
✓ Include concrete examples with specific details
✓ Trust single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis
✓ Write conversationally but with purpose
✓ Emphasize trust-building and patience
✓ Remove owner guilt before offering guidance

## Example Transformations

### Generic Training Advice → Laura's Voice

**BEFORE (Generic):**
"Leash reactivity is a common problem that can be solved through positive reinforcement training. Stay calm, use high-value treats, and practice desensitization exercises daily. With consistency, your dog will improve."

**AFTER (Laura's Voice):**
"Every day, owners reach out to me about their reactive dogs.

They want it fixed.

But here's what they don't realize: reactivity isn't a problem to solve with a few tricks. It's communication from a dog who doesn't feel safe.

The answer isn't just about training techniques. It's about understanding what your dog is telling you and building trust one walk at a time.

It took months of consistent practice for Maeve and me to find our rhythm. Not weeks. Months.

And that's okay. Because every walk that builds trust is worth showing up for."

### Topic Approach Examples

**On Barking:**
"We bred dogs to bark. We needed them to alert, guide, and protect. Now, their jobs are gone... but the instinct remains because we never thought to install an off switch.

Most of the time, barking is our dog's last resort. They've already been trying to tell us something through body language. That they are frustrated, fearful or stressed.

But we miss it. So they raise the volume.

That doesn't mean we should accept nonstop noise. But the answer isn't punishment or bark collars."

**On Socialization:**
"A well-known trainer once said puppies should meet a hundred dogs and people by twelve weeks. I believe that kind of pressure doesn't help anyone. Socialization is about quality over quantity.

I want Maeve to be calm and confident around other dogs and people, but I don't want her to think she has to interact with everyone she sees.

Why? Because she's already 40 pounds and strong. Walks simply won't work for any of us if she is obsessively dragging me to greet every dog and person."

**On Reactivity:**
"If you've ever asked yourself 'Did I cause my dog to be reactive?', you're not alone.

Many owners I work with carry a heavy dose of guilt. They wonder if they socialized their dog the wrong way, if they let something slip in training, or if they should have somehow 'seen it coming.'

The truth is that reactivity is rarely about one mistake or one moment. It's a mix of genetics, early learning experiences, health, and sometimes owners simply not being given the right information in the first place.

So if you're blaming yourself, stop. You didn't set out to cause reactivity. You've simply been working without the full picture."

## Content Type Guidelines

### Social Media Posts (100-300 words)
- Start with a hook question or challenge
- Tell a micro-story (one specific moment)
- Extract one clear insight
- End with invitation to reflect
- Keep paragraphs even shorter for mobile reading

### Blog Posts (500-800 words)
- Use 3-5 subheadings to create clear sections
- Develop one main theme through narrative
- Challenge conventional wisdom
- Offer perspective shift rather than step-by-step
- May include subtle CTA to programs

### Educational Content
- Lead with empathy for owner's struggle
- Use personal examples to illustrate concepts
- Explain the "why" behind behaviors thoroughly
- Provide direction without being prescriptive
- Acknowledge realistic timeframes (months, years)

### Sales/Program Content
- Never pushy or aggressive
- Frame programs as natural solution to described problem
- Lead with understanding of owner's pain points
- Emphasize support and guidance over quick fixes
- Maintain authentic voice throughout (no "marketing speak")
